1177 BC: The Year Civilization Collapsed

The 12th century BCE was a period of utter turmoil in the Eastern Mediterranean that saw the demise of nearly every complex society from Egypt to the Aegean. Dozens of theories attempt to explain but fail to fully interpret the fall of the civilized world as the period remains shrouded in horrific violence, shocking climate events, and intriguing diplomatic and personal narratives. Delegates will each represent a kingdom, city state, or independent polity as they attempt to work together to navigate the challenges of the 12th century. Delegates will be tasked with responding to calamitous natural disasters; collapse of established political dynasties; climate change, drought, and famine; invasions of foreign populations known collectively as 'The Sea People;' and globalizing economies and methods of warfare. While no previous knowledge of the time period is required, delegates who prepare well, grasp a firm historical perspective, and think quickly on their toes are most likely to successfully maneuver through the many challenges the committee will encounter. Will the committee be able to fortuitously and promptly respond to the hazards of the end of the bronze age or will they, true to history, face collapse?

Samuel Pfister

My name is Samuel Pfister; I’m currently a fourth year student studying ancient history and archaeology. I am also enrolled in the university’s MA program in anthropology where I’m studying museum collections of Near Eastern artifacts. Interested in museum curation, I am also an experienced field archaeologist conducting much of my fieldwork in Israel, currently as a staff member of the Kabri Archaeological Project.

I’ve been involved in Model UN since 6th grade, more than half my life. I have enjoyed seeing all of the ins and outs of the process, from being a delegate, staffer, chair, and secretariat member of various conference. I’m happy to once again be back in the committee room.

The idea for this committee came to me over some time. The author of the committee’s namesake book, Eric H. Cline, is my thesis dissertation advisor and mentor. I’m consistently inspired by his work and have to some degree followed in his footsteps in choosing my future. I see a strong intersection between the study of international affairs and history. Plagues through history are interactions between international entities. The Eastern Mediterranean region in 1177 was no exception. Here we will explore elements from natural disaster to refugee crises.

I truly hope you enjoy this committee as much as I will. My goal is for you all to come out of this will a new appreciation for ancient history, the intersection of international affairs, and a feeling of triumph having successfully navigated the collapse of civilization.
